# What is Cancer?

Cancer is a group of diseases characterized by uncontrolled cell growth, genetic mutations, and the ability of abnormal cells to invade surrounding tissues and spread throughout the body.

In healthy tissues, cells grow, divide, and die in a highly regulated manner. This regulation ensures that organs maintain proper structure and function. Cancer emerges when this regulatory system becomes disrupted.

The transformation of a normal cell into a cancerous one is typically driven by genetic alterations that affect key cellular processes such as:

- cell proliferation
- DNA repair
- programmed cell death (apoptosis)
- cellular differentiation
- immune recognition

These genetic alterations can accumulate over time due to environmental exposures, replication errors, or inherited mutations.

---

# Genetic Mutations and Cancer

Cancer development is closely linked to mutations in specific categories of genes:

## Oncogenes

Oncogenes are mutated or overexpressed versions of normal genes called proto-oncogenes. These genes normally regulate cell growth and division. When altered, they can drive uncontrolled proliferation.

Examples include genes involved in growth signaling pathways.

## Tumor Suppressor Genes

Tumor suppressor genes act as the cell's protective mechanisms. They regulate cell cycle checkpoints, repair DNA damage, and trigger apoptosis when necessary.

Loss or inactivation of tumor suppressor genes allows damaged cells to continue dividing.

## DNA Repair Genes

These genes maintain genomic stability by correcting errors that occur during DNA replication. When DNA repair mechanisms fail, mutations accumulate at a higher rate.

# Hallmarks of Cancer

Research has identified several common biological capabilities that many cancers acquire during their development. These are known as the "Hallmarks of Cancer".

They include:

- sustained proliferative signaling
- evading growth suppressors
- resisting cell death
- enabling replicative immortality
- inducing angiogenesis
- activating invasion and metastasis

These hallmarks describe how cancer cells gradually acquire abilities that allow them to survive, expand, and spread.

---

# Cancer and the Immune System

The immune system plays a critical role in recognizing and eliminating abnormal cells.

Immune cells such as T lymphocytes can detect certain molecular patterns expressed by tumor cells and initiate immune responses against them.

However, many cancers develop mechanisms to evade immune detection. These mechanisms include:

- suppressing immune activation
- expressing inhibitory signals
- modifying the tumor microenvironment

Understanding these immune evasion strategies is fundamental for the development of modern immunotherapies.

---

# Relevance for CAR-T Cell Therapy

CAR-T cell therapy is an advanced form of immunotherapy that engineers immune cells to better recognize and attack cancer cells.

This therapy modifies a patient's T cells so they can identify specific proteins (antigens) present on tumor cells.

Because cancer cells arise from the body's own tissues, distinguishing them from normal cells is a major challenge. Identifying safe and effective targets is therefore a central problem in CAR-T research.

Understanding the biological mechanisms that drive cancer development is essential for designing therapies capable of selectively targeting tumor cells.
