# T Cells

T cells are a central component of the adaptive immune system and play a critical role in identifying and eliminating infected or malignant cells.

They are particularly important in cancer immunology and form the biological foundation of CAR-T cell therapy.

---

# Origin and Development

T cells originate from hematopoietic stem cells in the bone marrow and mature in the thymus.

During maturation, T cells undergo a selection process to ensure:

- recognition of foreign antigens  
- tolerance to self-antigens  

This process is essential to prevent autoimmunity while maintaining effective immune responses.

---

# Types of T Cells

T cells can be broadly classified into several subtypes based on their function:

## CD8+ Cytotoxic T Cells

- directly kill infected or cancerous cells  
- recognize antigens presented by MHC Class I molecules  

These cells are the primary effectors in tumor cell elimination.

---

## CD4+ Helper T Cells

- coordinate immune responses  
- recognize antigens presented by MHC Class II molecules  

They support other immune cells by releasing cytokines.

---

## CD4+ Helper T Cell Subtypes

CD4+ T cells differentiate into specialized subsets:

- Th1: promotes cellular immunity (critical for anti-tumor response)  
- Th2: supports humoral immunity  
- Th17: involved in inflammation  
- Tfh: assists B cell activation  

Each subtype is driven by specific cytokine environments.

---

## Regulatory T Cells (Tregs)

- suppress immune responses  
- maintain immune tolerance  
- prevent autoimmunity  

However, they can also inhibit anti-tumor immunity.

---

# T Cell Receptors (TCR)

T cells recognize antigens through T-cell receptors (TCRs).

Key characteristics:

- highly specific recognition  
- require antigen presentation via MHC  
- essential for adaptive immunity  

This dependence on MHC distinguishes natural T cell responses from CAR-T cells.

---

# T Cell Activation

T cell activation requires multiple signals:

## Signal 1: Antigen Recognition

The TCR binds to an antigen presented by MHC on antigen-presenting cells.

---

## Signal 2: Costimulation

Additional signals are required for full activation, typically involving:

- CD28 receptor  
- interaction with costimulatory molecules  

---

## Signal 3: Cytokine Signaling

Cytokines influence T cell differentiation, proliferation, and function.

---

Without proper costimulation, T cells may become inactive (anergy).

---

# Intracellular Signaling

T cell activation triggers intracellular signaling cascades, including:

- MAPK pathway  
- NF-κB pathway  
- PI3K/AKT pathway  

These pathways regulate:

- proliferation  
- survival  
- effector functions  

They are directly relevant for CAR-T design, particularly in selecting signaling domains such as CD28 or 4-1BB.

---

# Immune Checkpoints

T cell activity is regulated by inhibitory receptors known as immune checkpoints.

Key examples include:

- PD-1  
- CTLA-4  

These molecules limit T cell activation to prevent excessive immune responses.

Tumors exploit these pathways to suppress T cell function and promote immune evasion.

---

# Clonal Expansion

Once activated, T cells undergo rapid proliferation.

This process, known as clonal expansion, generates a large number of T cells specific to a particular antigen.

This amplification is essential for an effective immune response.

---

# Effector Functions

Activated T cells perform various functions:

## Cytotoxic Activity

CD8+ T cells kill target cells by releasing:

- perforin (creates pores in target cells)  
- granzymes (induce apoptosis)  

---

## Cytokine Production

T cells release cytokines that:

- regulate immune responses  
- recruit other immune cells  
- enhance inflammation  

---

# Tumor Microenvironment (TME)

T cell function is heavily influenced by the tumor microenvironment.

The TME can include:

- immunosuppressive cells (e.g., Tregs, myeloid-derived suppressor cells)  
- inhibitory cytokines  
- hypoxic conditions  

These factors impair T cell activity and promote exhaustion.

---

# T Cell Memory

After an immune response, some T cells become memory cells.

These cells provide:

- faster response upon re-exposure  
- long-term immunity  

Memory T cells are crucial for sustained protection.

---

# T Cell Exhaustion

In chronic conditions such as cancer, T cells may become exhausted.

Characteristics include:

- reduced function  
- decreased cytokine production  
- impaired proliferation  

T cell exhaustion is a major limitation in anti-tumor immunity and is closely linked to immune checkpoint activity and the tumor microenvironment.

---

# T Cells in Cancer

T cells play a critical role in recognizing and eliminating tumor cells.

However, tumors can:

- evade detection  
- suppress T cell activity  
- induce exhaustion  

This limits the effectiveness of natural immune responses against cancer.

---

# T Cells and CAR-T Therapy

CAR-T therapy is based on engineering T cells to enhance their anti-tumor activity.

Key differences between natural T cells and CAR-T cells:

- CAR-T cells recognize antigens independently of MHC  
- CAR-T cells are genetically modified for improved targeting  
- CAR-T cells can incorporate optimized intracellular signaling domains  
- CAR-T cells can partially overcome tumor immune evasion mechanisms
